Author: Umesh Vanka

## Abstract

In recent years, contrastive learning (CL) frameworks have been widely applied to multivariate time series classification (MTSC) tasks. However, existing methods lack taskspecific guidance, leading to limitations in fully capturing the complex dynamics and invariant representations in time series data. Motivated by the auxiliary tasks in multitask learning (MTL) and to fully utilize the rich frequency-domain information of time series data, we propose a novel time series classification framework, uncertainty-based time-frequency supervised CL (UTFSCL). This framework uses SCL in the time and frequency domains and time-frequency consistency as auxiliary tasks to improve the primary task of using only instance-level labels for time series classification. Furthermore, inspired by the homogeneous uncertainty in MTL, we derive a novel uncertainty loss function, which automatically adjusts the weights according to the degree of uncertainty of di GLYPH&lt;11&gt; erent tasks to optimize the learning and prediction process of the model. The proposed framework is evaluated on MTSC tasks, including human activity recognition (HAR), air writing, and gesture recognition. In addition, we create a human-drone interaction (HDI) dataset consisting of 20 subjects and conduct real-world experiments to evaluate the proposed framework. The extensive experiments conducted in various settings verify the e GLYPH&lt;11&gt; ectiveness of the proposed framework.

Index Terms -Multitask learning (MTL), multivariate time series classification (MTSC), supervised contrastive learning (SCL).

## I. INTRODUCTION

T IME series data exist in many real-world scenarios, including wind forecasting [1], stock forecasting [2], human activity recognition (HAR) [3], industrial fault diagnosis [4], [5], and healthcare [6]. Such continuous data represent states or events at points in time, making them ideal for analyzing trends, patterns, and anomalies. The key to analyzing time series data is to extract the information-rich features for in-depth analysis and decision support.

Multivariate time series classification (MTSC), which focuses on predicting category labels of ordered real-valued data, can be divided into traditional nondeep learning methods and advanced deep learning methods. Most traditional methods

However, in certain instances, they lack task-specific guidance, resulting in suboptimal performance when applied directly to specific downstream tasks. To overcome this limitation, incorporating labels from existing datasets is an e GLYPH&lt;11&gt; ective strategy. ## II. RELATED WORKS

## A. Time-Frequency Domain Fusion for Time Series

Incorporating prior knowledge into models can significantly improve feature extraction, enhance generalization, and increase robustness to noise [28]. Frequency-domain features, derived via Fourier transformations, serve as e GLYPH&lt;11&gt; ective prior knowledge, providing a global perspective that complements time-domain data [24], [26], [29], [30]. The key is to e GLYPH&lt;11&gt; ectively integrate these two domains to leverage their complementary strengths. One intuitive approach is signallevel fusion via the short-time Fourier transform (STFT), which transforms time series into 2-D spectrograms, thereby incorporating time-frequency information into model inputs [31]. Fusion can also occur at the feature level, for example, Jin and Chen [32] proposed an end-to-end deep learning framework that employs time-frequency analysis to extract features from vibration signals and combine them into input embeddings for transformers. Furthermore, tight feature alignment can be achieved by learning the consistency between timeand frequency-domain features through cross-domain CL [29] and by learning the domain independence through intradomain CL [5].

## B. CL for Time Series

CL has emerged as a powerful self-supervised framework for learning representations by maximizing similarity between positive pairs and minimizing dissimilarity between negative pairs. CL is extensively used in many fields, including image classification [22], video analysis [33], HAR [34], remote photoplethysmography (rPPG) [35], and time series analysis [18], [23], [24]. Data augmentation is crucial in a CL-based framework; unlike traditional data augmentation in computer vision, time series augmentation must consider temporal dependencies. For instance, TCL [36] splits the time series into nonoverlapping windows and treats each window as an independent view, exploiting the properties of nonstationary time series by assuming negative pairs for distant windows and positive pairs for neighboring windows. ## III. PRINCIPLE AND METHOD

## A. Overview of the Proposed U-TFSCL Framework

To fully exploit features across the time-frequency domains in the time series data, we propose the U-TFSCL framework, as shown in Fig. 1. This framework comprises a parallel encoder module operating within both the time and frequency domains, a time-frequency domain SCL (TFSCL) module, and an MTL module that balances the loss based on task uncertainty. These components aim to provide a comprehensive and e GLYPH&lt;11&gt; ective approach for analyzing time series data. Specifically, the original time-domain signals are input into the U-TFSCL framework, where they are first converted to their frequency-domain counterparts using a rapid Fourier transform. These original and transformed signals are then fed to parallel branches for time- and frequency-domain processing. Within each branch, advanced data augmentation methods are employed to generate positive and negative sample pairs. Subsequently, these pairs are processed using independent encoder modules. These modules consist of several transformer blocks and extract distinct time- and frequency-domain features from the pairs.

Subsequently, the extracted features are fed into the TFSCL module, which uses SCL to calculate intradomain and interdomain losses and improve the performance of the classification task by using the intradomain and interdomain comparison tasks as auxiliary tasks. In this module, the timeand frequency-domain losses are calculated first. Then, the features that calculate the intradomain loss are projected into a shared latent space by separate projection layers. In this space, the time-frequency domain consistency loss is calculated.

## C. MTL Based on Homoscedastic Uncertainty

When constructing the total loss function L total, it is necessary to consider several parameters that require careful tuning.

The optimal setting of these parameters is critical for the model's performance. However, traditional methods of parameter tuning, such as those relying on experience, intuition, or complex grid searches, are often di GLYPH&lt;14&gt; cult and expensive, making it di GLYPH&lt;14&gt; cult to find an optimal global solution. To address this problem, we propose a novel uncertainty-based approach. L total is treated as a collection of di GLYPH&lt;11&gt; erent tasks with di GLYPH&lt;11&gt; erent but competing goals, and the uncertainty of these tasks is used to automatically learn how to assign weights to them.

In Bayesian modeling, two main types of uncertainty can be modeled: epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty. Epistemic uncertainty reflects the model's own uncertainty of the data, whereas aleatoric uncertainty describes the inherent noise and variability in the data. Understanding this form of uncertainty is critical to capturing the intricate connections between tasks and their respective roles in the learning process of a model. ## D. Development and Features of the HDI Gesture Dataset

To complement the existing MTSC dataset, based on previous gesture HDI studies [43], [44], we designed six gestures for a challenging gesture-based HDI task: (G1) Move Left, (G2) Move Right, (G3) Hover, (G4) Move Up, (G5) Move Down, and (G6) Move Forward; Fig. 3 illustrates the movement of each gesture. In this task, participants were required to control drone flight trajectories through six predefined hand gestures while wearing an inertial measurement unit (IMU) sensor on their dominant hand. All participants started the experiment without prior training on the target gestures to preserve interindividual variability.

We obtained data from 20 participants (14 males and six females) to construct the gesture dataset; the participants have an average age of 22.6 ( ± 2.9) and mostly had backgrounds in computer science. All participants are right-handed. Participants repeated each gesture approximately 30 times after a 3-s preparation period for each gesture. Recorded six-channel signals (accelerometer and gyroscope) during executions are transmitted at 200 Hz via Bluetooth low energy (BLE) to a ground station.

In order to improve the e GLYPH&lt;14&gt; ciency of labeling and to obtain more high-quality labeled data, we adopted a semiautomatic data labeling method that consisted of two phases:

## IV. EXPERIMENT

## A. Experimental Settings

## V. CONCLUSION

In this study, a novel framework U-TFSCL was proposed with higher classification accuracy to solve the MTSC problem and construct a real-world gesture dataset of HDIs to evaluate the proposed framework.

The main contribution of this study is the design of two key modules: the time-frequency contrastive module TFSCL, which uses timeand frequency-domain SCL as auxiliary tasks; and the MTL module, which is driven by task uncertainty. These modules are rationally organized in the proposed framework. After detailed and extensive experiments, the following conclusions are drawn.

- 1) The combination of time- and frequency-domain information significantly improved the performance of the model. The results of the ablation experiments on the TFSCL module show that the model using both types of information significantly outperformed the model using only one modality.
- 2) The number of labels used in CL a GLYPH&lt;11&gt; ects the number of positive pairs per anchor, which in turn influences the performance of the model. When using the full set of labels in the TFSCL module, the model achieves optimal performance.
- 3) The MTL module dynamically adjusted the task weights based on task uncertainty, which not only improved performance but also avoided the cumbersome network search process, thus determining the approximate weight combinations more e GLYPH&lt;14&gt; ciently.

Future work will further explore the applicability of the proposed framework to a wide range of real-world time series classification applications and evaluate its performance in terms of domain adaptation. This framework provides new perspectives and more e GLYPH&lt;14&gt; cient solutions in the field of time series analysis.
